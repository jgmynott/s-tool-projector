Methodology

How s-tool builds honest projection bands.

Projections anyone can trust — because the engine shows its own error bar, and we're ruthless about what gets in and what gets killed.

1. The projection engine

Every ticker runs through a proprietary stochastic projection model that learns the stock's own return and volatility behavior from several years of daily closes. The engine simulates a large number of forward paths through the stock's volatility regime, anchored by a fundamental-reversion pull so projections don't drift unrealistically far from their long-run gravity.

For the asymmetric-upside sleeve we layer a volatility-regime-adaptive variant of the same engine on top — one that weights recent market conditions more heavily, widening the forecast tail when something is actually cooking under the hood.

At the end, the engine reports the standard percentile bands: P10 / P90 (outer 80%), P25 / P75 (interior 50%), and P50 as the center of gravity, not a price target.

2. Honesty via out-of-sample error

Every projection on s-tool ships with the model's historical error on that specific ticker — computed by walking the engine forward through hundreds of historical dates and comparing its P50 call to what actually happened. The math is standard walk-forward cross-validation; the discipline is in showing the result, not hiding it.

A 15% error band on a 40%-vol stock is honest. A 2% error band would be a lie. We don't give you the 2% number.

The engine shows its error bar on every projection. When bands are wide, the stock is genuinely hard to predict — and you'll see it immediately. No hidden confidence.

3. The signal graveyard

Before any tilt, factor, or sentiment overlay ships to production, it has to pass a strict gate: measurable improvement across thousands of symbols and years of rolling backtests. Not one-period wins, not cherry-picked charts.

We've tested roughly two dozen candidate signals — crowd sentiment, text-derived attention metrics, macroeconomic regime signals, credit spreads, options skew, insider-transaction flows, and more. Almost all of them were either arbitraged away in 2023 (as LLM-generated content polluted text corpora) or never had measurable edge to begin with.

The public signal graveyard is long. The engine's active signal list is short on purpose.

4. The engine self-tunes overnight

Every night, after projections refresh, a self-training scoring loop re-evaluates active scoring methods against the most recent out-of-sample windows. Whichever method has been producing the best realized-return hit rate in the current market regime gets promoted to production. If regime shifts and the winning method stops working, a different one takes over — automatically, transparently.

Hypothesis: Text-based and macro-regime signals are over-discovered. Real edge increasingly lives in regulatory-disclosure data, in volatility-regime detection, and in cross-sectional behavior — where incentive structures or structural frictions keep arbitrageurs from closing the gap quickly.

5. The bar for production

A signal makes it into the engine only when it clears three thresholds:

  1. Economic rationale — why should this work? If we can't write it in one paragraph, it's pattern-matching.
  2. Out-of-sample robustness — does it hold across multiple regimes (2018 bull, 2020 dislocation, 2022 bear, 2023–24 recovery) and thousands of symbols?
  3. Measurable lift over the baseline engine — not a p-value trick; a material improvement in out-of-sample error or in realized-return hit rates.

Signals that don't clear all three get published in the graveyard, not in the engine.

6. The portfolio layer

We run the engine nightly across the broad US universe and publish four ranked tiers: Conservative · Moderate · Aggressive · Asymmetric. The first three bucket by projected volatility with a positive-return filter and fundamentals-quality overlay. The Asymmetric sleeve ranks by upside-tail probability under the volatility-adaptive model.

Every pick is appended to an immutable ledger on pick date. Realized returns get computed against the entry price every night. That ledger is what lets you audit whether the engine earns its keep.

7. Data foundation

We don't invent data. The engine is fed from established sources:

  • Price history — standard daily OHLCV across the US equity universe, refreshed nightly.
  • Regulatory filings — point-in-time fundamentals derived from XBRL filings.
  • Institutional positioning — consolidated short-interest and capital-commitment signals.
  • Macroeconomic reference — for volatility-regime context and tail-risk calibration.

Signal engineering — the transformation from raw data into something predictive — is the proprietary piece. The data itself is table stakes.
